Every project manager has a story about the one that almost got away. The project that, by all logical measures, should have failed. For me, that project was Risk Reporting & Integrations 2025, and for the longest time, I’ve told myself we just got lucky.

Looking back, I realize it was something more.

It was early March, and I was already juggling a full portfolio, including a high-stakes CyberArk implementation and a complex Active Directory project. Then, I was handed RRI25. The circumstances were unusual, and I walked into the first technical meeting on Day 2 with more questions than answers.

Within 45 minutes, the project’s neat and tidy schedule was in flames. We were hit with a perfect storm:

  • Our single most critical Subject Matter Expert was unexpectedly out on a medical emergency.

  • We confirmed there was no test environment for a core system—all work had to be done in Production.

  • A non-negotiable security mandate from a key stakeholder invalidated our entire technical plan.

  • We uncovered a hidden compliance minefield of regulated data that put hard boundaries on what we could build.

I remember thinking, “How do we possibly survive this?” The project had every classic ingredient for failure, and we were only 48 hours in. The logical assessment was clear: this project should have derailed.

We delivered it successfully. And for months, my go-to explanation has been, “Boy, were we lucky.”

But as I’ve reflected on that project—I’ve started to see that “luck” was actually a series of deliberate choices made by a team under incredible pressure. What felt like dodging bullets was, in reality, a masterclass in crisis navigation that I was too deep in the storm to recognize at the time.

Where “Luck” Was Actually Skill in Disguise

It’s hard to judge your own actions in the heat of the moment. You’re just trying to keep things moving. But looking back, I can see the critical pivots the team made that had nothing to do with luck.

1. We felt “lucky” that the security mandate didn’t kill the project.
In reality, the team didn’t get lucky; we got pragmatic. When the platform team mandated OAuth 2.0 and we discovered the target system couldn’t support it, we didn’t give up. We investigated, found a viable and secure alternative (API Keys), and negotiated a compromise. We respected the stakeholder’s intent while providing a realistic path forward. That wasn’t luck; that was data-driven negotiation.

2. We felt “lucky” that our key SME returned.
While his return was crucial, the team didn’t just wait passively. We immediately activated our backup SME. We kept the project moving on the questions we could answer and meticulously documented the ones we couldn’t. We built a plan that could function in the SME’s absence, making his return an accelerator, not a prerequisite for survival. That wasn’t luck; that was resilient resource management.

3. We felt “lucky” that we didn’t break the production environment.
The risk of working in a production-only system was enormous. But the team didn’t just cross their fingers. We confronted the risk head-on, secured formal acceptance from the business owners, and collaboratively designed a safe testing protocol. We created guardrails where none existed. That wasn’t luck; that was proactive risk mitigation.

The Real Definition of a “Lucky” Project

I still believe there’s an element of luck in any successful project. You can’t control everything. But RRI25 taught me a profound lesson.

A project isn’t “lucky” because it avoids problems. A project is “lucky” when it has a team with the courage to face those problems head-on. It’s “lucky” when it has leaders who can create clarity from chaos, and stakeholders who are willing to engage in pragmatic problem-solving.

It’s not all about one person. It was the collective effort: the platform owner who held a firm line on security, the technical lead who investigated the impossible mandate, and the business owners who formally accepted the environmental risks.

I may have been the one steering the ship, but the entire crew was bailing water, patching holes, and helping me read the compass.

So yes, I still think we were lucky. We were lucky to have a team that, when faced with every reason to fail, chose to lead instead. And that, I’ve learned, is the kind of luck you have to make for yourself.
