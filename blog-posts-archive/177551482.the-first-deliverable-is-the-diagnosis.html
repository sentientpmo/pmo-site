When a senior leader is brought in to lead a new, high-stakes initiative—especially a turnaround—the pressure to immediately “start doing things” is immense. The sponsor wants to see action. The team wants direction. The temptation is to jump in and start managing the existing plan.

This is almost always a mistake.

After decades of being deployed into the most complex and chaotic projects, I’ve learned that the most critical phase is the one that happens before you ever touch the Gantt chart. It’s The Diagnostic.

You cannot solve a problem you don’t fully understand. My first 30 days on any new engagement are dedicated to this single purpose. My first deliverable is not a status report or a revised schedule; it is the Diagnosis—a clear, unvarnished, and evidence-based assessment of the project’s true state.

This isn’t about blaming the past; it’s about architecting a realistic future. Here is the framework for a powerful diagnostic.

1. Map the Human Terrain (The Political Diagnosis)
Before you look at a single project artifact, you must understand the human systems at play.

  • The Listening Tour: I conduct a blitz of short, one-on-one interviews with everyone from the executive sponsor to the most junior engineer. I ask two questions: “What is working?” and “What is broken?”

  • The Output: I create a confidential stakeholder map that identifies the true centers of influence, the hidden fault lines, and the competing agendas. This political blueprint is often more important than the technical one.

2. Pressure-Test the “Ground Truth” (The Reality Diagnosis)
The official project documents often reflect a reality that no longer exists.

  • The Artifact Review: I compare the project charter and the initial business case to the current RAID log and the latest meeting minutes. Where are the disconnects? Has the scope shifted? Have the risks been updated?

  • The Output: I produce a “Plan vs. Reality” summary. It’s a simple, powerful document that highlights the gap between the original promise and the current situation. For example, on the RRI25 project, we discovered on Day 2 that the authentication method in the plan was technically impossible—a critical disconnect.

3. Identify the “Bleeding Neck” (The Triage Diagnosis)
Every troubled project has a single, urgent problem that is causing the most pain.

  • The Pain Point Analysis: Based on the listening tour, I identify the one blocker that, if solved, would unlock the most progress and build the most momentum. It’s rarely the biggest problem, but it’s the most immediate.

  • The Output: I present a “First 90-Day Triage Plan” that is relentlessly focused on solving this single “bleeding neck” problem first. This builds immediate credibility and earns the right to lead the longer-term transformation.

The Lesson: You Must Go Slow to Go Fast

The pressure to act immediately is immense, but premature action on a faulty diagnosis is the leading cause of project failure. By having the discipline to dedicate the first 30 days to a rigorous Diagnostic, you do more than just create a better plan.

You establish yourself as a leader who is thoughtful, data-driven, and in control. You build trust with your stakeholders by showing them you understand their reality. And you ensure that when you finally do act, your actions are precise, impactful, and aimed at solving the right problem.

The Diagnosis is not a delay. It is the most valuable and time-saving deliverable you will ever produce.
