Over the decades, I’ve seen something that always puzzled me: brilliant, intelligent people who just never seem to get the wind in their sails. They work, they analyze, they have incredible ideas, but they remain stuck, never quite achieving the impact they’re capable of.

They’re caught in what I call “The Intelligence Trap.”

The trap is believing that being smart is the same as being effective. It’s the mistake of confusing intellectual activity with tangible achievement. It’s a place where ideas are debated endlessly, but decisions are never made; where plans are perfected, but work never begins.

I was fortunate to avoid that trap, not because I was smarter, but because my definition of “hard work” was different. It was never about just putting in the hours; it was about applying that effort to create momentum and deliver results.

If you feel like you’re spinning your wheels or want to help others break free, here is the most important advice I can share from my career of turning chaos into outcomes.

1. Stop Confusing Activity with Achievement

The single biggest mistake a smart person can make is falling in love with the complexity of the problem. They’ll build the perfect model, design the flawless system architecture, and analyze every possible edge case. This feels incredibly productive, but it produces nothing.

Your goal is not to be the most knowledgeable person in the room; it’s to get the room to the finish line.

How to avoid the trap: Relentlessly focus on the outcome. Before you start any task, ask the simple question: “Will this activity get us closer to solving the business problem?” At TD Ameritrade, the project wasn’t to create a perfect technology design; the project was to double the trading floor’s capacity. That singular focus allowed us to cut through the noise, recover a failing program, and deliver 45 days ahead of schedule. Always tie your work to the real-world result.

2. Be the Architect of Clarity

Smart people can thrive in ambiguity, but organizations cannot. When I was brought in to turn around failing programs at MetLife or Guardian Life, the problem was never a lack of smart people. The problem was a lack of structure. There was no clear framework for making decisions, managing risk, or measuring progress.

The most valuable “hard work” you can do is to build the system that allows others to succeed. Be the person who creates clarity in the midst of chaos.

How to avoid the trap: Build the framework. This is your sail and your rudder. It can be as simple as a well-defined project plan or as robust as establishing an entire Program Management Office (PMO). When you provide a clear process, defined roles, and a common language, you empower the entire team to move in the same direction. Structure isn’t bureaucracy; it’s the machine that turns raw effort into forward momentum.

3. Master the Last Mile

Anyone can get excited about a new project. The kickoff meeting is full of energy and big ideas. But where 90% of initiatives die is in the final, grueling 10%—the “last mile.” This is where the boring work happens, where unexpected problems emerge, and where discipline truly matters.

The Intelligence Trap tells you to move on to the next, more interesting intellectual puzzle. True achievers, however, know that the victory is in finishing.

How to avoid the trap: Fall in love with “done.” At PNC Bank, we were facing a massive regulatory mandate with hundreds of millions of dollars on the line. The hard work wasn’t just designing the risk assessment program; it was executing over 2,000 asset reviews and delivering the entire program five weeks ahead of schedule. Cultivate the grit to push through the tedious, repetitive, and difficult tasks required to see a project to its absolute completion. That is where the real value is unlocked.

4. Become a Translator

The final piece of the trap is poor communication. A brilliant idea is worthless if you can’t get anyone to understand it, believe in it, or fund it. Throughout my career, the most critical role I’ve played was serving as the “central interpreter between business and IT.”

You cannot hide behind your technical expertise. Your responsibility is to bridge the gap between the complexity of the work and the business value it delivers.

How to avoid the trap: Speak the language of “so what?” Don’t just report on what you did; explain why it matters. Author the documents—the vision, the test strategy, the governance plan—that make the path forward clear for everyone. Secure buy-in not by being the smartest person in the room, but by being the most understood.

The Real Work

Success isn’t about your IQ. It’s about your bias for action and your commitment to delivering tangible results. It’s about having the discipline to build the sail, the grit to steer through the storm, and the clarity to know which port you’re sailing for.

Don’t aspire to be the smartest person in the room. Aspire to be the person who gets things done. That is the work that truly matters.
