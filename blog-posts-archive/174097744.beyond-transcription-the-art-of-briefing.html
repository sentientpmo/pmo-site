We're all having the same conversation right now. A colleague leans in, lowers their voice, and asks, "Have you tried using AI for meeting minutes?"

The answer is an emphatic "yes," followed by a story of how it's a game-changer, a productivity unlock, a way to finally escape the administrative grind.

And it's all true. Using an AI to transcribe and summarize your meetings is the new table stakes for any serious program manager. It's a fantastic first step.

But it’s just that—a first step.

The common refrain I hear next is, "Of course, it's not perfect. You have to proofread it. It gets the names wrong sometimes." We treat these flaws as an acceptable cost of convenience.

I believe that's the wrong way to look at it. The AI's mistakes aren't a flaw in the technology; they're a flaw in our method. We are treating a powerful intelligence like a simple transcriber. We are giving it a task without giving it a briefing.

When you onboard a new junior project manager, you don't just hand them a recording and say, "Tell me what happened." You give them the project charter and the stakeholder list. You brief them so they can understand not just what was said, but why it matters.

We must do the same for our AI co-pilots.

Level 1: The Briefing Dossier - Eliminating "Contextual Amnesia"

For my most complex programs, I’ve stopped just dropping a transcript into a prompt window. I now provide a comprehensive "Briefing Dossier" along with the transcript. It transforms the AI from a passive listener into a knowledgeable participant for a single event.

Here is the three-part dossier that gets me near-perfect summaries every time.

1. The "Ground Truth" Snapshot (The Roster):
The easiest way to fix the "wrong names" problem is to prevent it. My hack: I take a screenshot of the MS Teams or Zoom invitation. This image contains the critical metadata: the exact meeting title, date, and attendee list. I feed this to the AI first. Boom. The AI has its official roster.

2. The Project Context (The "One-Pager"):
Next, I provide a "one-pager" on the project's soul. What is the objective? What are the key workstreams? What are the primary risks? This tells the AI what to listen for. For example, on "Project Apex," the AI is briefed that any mention of "API rate limiting" is a high-risk item to be flagged.

3. The Project Lexicon (The "Jargon Buster"):
Every team has its own shorthand. I provide a glossary of our common acronyms and terms (e.g., DWH, ETL, UAT, SteerCo). This eliminates 90% of transcription errors and makes the final summary instantly readable.

Level 2: The Context Package - Giving Your AI a Memory

The Briefing Dossier is fantastic for getting a perfect summary of a single meeting. But its knowledge is isolated. It suffers from "Contextual Amnesia," with no memory of what was decided on Monday when it's summarizing Tuesday's call.

The real breakthrough happens when you stop giving your AI a briefing and start giving it a memory.

Before I ask my AI to summarize a critical meeting, I now provide it with a curated "Context Package"—a collection of recent, relevant project artifacts. Depending on the meeting, this might include:

  • The entire RAID Log as a text file.

  • The executive summaries from the last three weekly meetings.

  • The last two status reports sent to the Steering Committee.

I feed the AI this package along with the new meeting transcript. The prompt is no longer just "summarize this." It's "summarize this new transcript in the context of these recent events."

From Summary to True Synthesis

This is where the magic happens. The AI is no longer just a scribe. It becomes a junior analyst. It can now connect the dots across time.

When you provide the full Dossier (Snapshot + Context + Lexicon) and the Context Package (RAID Log, past summaries), the AI's output is no longer a mere summary. It’s a work of true synthesis.

  • It doesn't just note a new action item; it can flag that this action item contradicts a decision made last week.

  • It doesn't just summarize a discussion about a technical issue; it can cross-reference it with the RAID log and ask, "Is this related to Risk #117?"

  • It doesn't just capture a new timeline commitment; it can compare it to the last status report and highlight, "This represents a 2-week slip from the previously communicated date."

The future of work isn't just about using AI. It's about learning how to collaborate with it. Our most valuable new skill is context curation—knowing which documents to provide to give our AI the memory it needs to generate the deepest insights. We are moving past simple automation and building a true partnership with an intelligence that can remember, correlate, and reason across the entire lifecycle of our projects.
