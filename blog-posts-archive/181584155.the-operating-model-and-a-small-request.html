If you’ve been reading what I’ve published here for a while, you’ve probably noticed a pattern.

Much of my writing has circled the same failure modes: decision latency, administrative drag, “green” programs that are quietly red underneath, and the widening gap between how work actually happens and how leadership experiences it.

None of those pieces were meant to stand alone. They were field notes—written while solving real delivery problems in real environments.

At a certain point, though, I realized something important: when there are too many fragments, clarity degrades instead of improving.

Signal turns into noise. Readers are left to infer the operating model. And inference is where execution starts to fail.

So I made a deliberate decision to consolidate.

Not to retreat from the ideas. Not to sanitize them. But to assemble the system they were pointing toward.

That consolidation resulted in a short book: The Sentient PMO: Architecting Clarity in the Age of AI.

[Link to Amazon Page]

It’s not a collection of articles, and it’s not a manifesto. It’s the operating model itself—how I approach clarity, risk, and decision-making when the stakes are high and the tolerance for drift is low.
