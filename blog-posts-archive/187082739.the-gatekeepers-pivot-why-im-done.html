When you start moving at high velocity—when you’re building something that actually threatens the status quo of a multi-billion dollar industry—the weeds start sprouting almost overnight.

I’ve spent the last few weeks sprinting. We’re deploying AI to dismantle project narratives and surfacing the “Truth Gap” in capital programs. But as the signal gets louder, so does the noise.

I’ve realized something: I’m not good at weeding. I’ve never had to be. But if you don’t clear the field, the parasites will choke the engine.

I’ve identified three types of “Industry Weeds” that every high-growth founder needs to recognize and delete immediately.

1. The Rent-Seeker (The “Taxman”)

This is the person who sees your Ferrari and tries to sell you a “speed limit” sticker. I recently dealt with a veteran who wanted to “collaborate.” The moment he saw the power of the tech, he tried to pivot from a guest judge to a licensing partner. He wanted to “tax” my innovation with trailing royalties for his “governance notes.”

The Lesson: If they want to be in the driver’s seat of a car they didn’t build, they aren’t a partner—they’re a hitchhiker looking for a payday.

2. The Ghost (The “Amateur”)

I had a commissioning lead reach out. “Let’s sync, huge alignment, I want to see the protocol.” We set the time. Show time comes. Nothing. No email, no cancellation, no “something came up.” Just a blank Zoom room.

In this industry, your word is your bond, and your calendar is your reputation. If you can’t manage a 20-minute calendar invite, you sure as hell can’t manage a $100M project recovery.

The Lesson: Ghosting isn’t just a lack of manners; it’s a lack of rigor. It’s an immediate, permanent disqualification.

3. The Brain-Picker (The “Vampire”)

Then there’s the ex-Big Four consultant. “I’d love to meet to pick your brain.”

Think about that phrase. It’s purely extractive. They want to download $50,000 worth of IP and 20 years of “Forensic Scars” over a cup of coffee (that they probably won’t even pay for). I gave one the benefit of the doubt, thinking maybe there was a network play. I sent the link. Result? Total silence.

The Lesson: I am not a public library. My “brain” is the R&D department of a high-value firm. If you want access to it, you either bring a checkbook or an equivalent exchange of value.

The New Standard: Pre-Qualification or GTFO

I’ve learned my lesson. My calendar is no longer an open door—it’s a secure vault.

From here on out, we are moving to Instrument Flight Rules. If you want a piece of the Sentient roadmap, you have to prove you belong in the room.

  1. No “Brain Picking”: If you don’t have a specific problem to solve or a specific value to offer, the answer is “Read the Substack.”

  2. Mandatory Pre-Qualification: If you want a slot, you’ll be filling out a diagnostic first. If the logic doesn’t hold water, the link doesn’t get sent.

  3. The “Theater” Test: If you’re more interested in the “narrative” of innovation than the “physics” of the work, stay in the cheap seats.

I’m built for the 60-day sprint. I don’t have time to teach “Introduction to Integrity” to people who are just looking for a hustle or a free lunch.

The “Sentient” model isn’t just about auditing projects; it’s about auditing the people we let into the ecosystem. The weeds are being cleared.

Let’s get back to the physics.
