In the world of digital transformation, the real work happens where the new meets the old. My last project was a perfect example: integrating a modern, cloud-based ServiceNow GRC platform with a critical, on-premise ticketing system. The goal was strategic: automate cybersecurity compliance, reduce hundreds of hours of manual effort, and create a single, unified view of our risk landscape.

The journey, however, was anything but a straight line. Integrating a modern SaaS platform with an established internal system was a masterclass in navigating technical roadblocks, ambiguous processes, and resource constraints.

Here are the three biggest lessons we learned along the way—lessons that apply to any complex technology project.

Lesson 1: Adapt Your Technical Strategy, Not Your Goal

No project plan survives contact with reality, especially when it comes to legacy system integration. We hit our first major technical hurdle when we discovered our modern authentication standard (OAuth 2.0) wasn’t compatible with the ticketing system’s API. A second challenge quickly followed: direct cloud-to-on-premise connectivity was blocked by internal firewalls.

The goal was integration, not a specific protocol or network path. Instead of getting stuck, we pivoted.

  • The Authentication Hurdle: We shifted from OAuth 2.0 to using secure API keys. This met our immediate security and functionality requirements, allowing the project to move forward while we logged the OAuth 2.0 upgrade as a future enhancement for the platform’s next major release.

  • The Connectivity Block: Instead of a lengthy and complex firewall change process, we implemented a Mid Server—a secure intermediary that could communicate with both the cloud platform and the internal system without requiring major network changes.

The Takeaway: When a technical roadblock appears, don’t be afraid to adapt your approach. By focusing on the ultimate goal—secure and reliable data exchange—we found pragmatic solutions that kept the project on schedule without compromising on security or functionality. This flexibility is crucial for maintaining momentum.

Lesson 2: Proactive Risk Management Turns Roadblocks into Speedbumps

Our decision to use API keys solved one problem but created a new, potential one: How would these keys be managed and rotated over time? If we didn’t address this, we risked a future integration failure or a security gap.

This is where a formal RAID (Risks, Assumptions, Issues, and Dependencies) log becomes a project manager’s best friend. Instead of waiting for the problem to surface after go-live, we flagged it as a high-priority operational risk.

Our mitigation plan was simple but effective:

  1. Define the Process: We documented a clear, manual Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for the quarterly API key rotation.

  2. Assign Ownership: We worked with stakeholders to formally assign ownership of the process to a specific operational team.

  3. Train for Handoff: We included a detailed “how-to” session as part of the knowledge transfer to ensure the new owners were prepared from day one.

We applied the same proactive approach to resource risks, like the availability of Subject Matter Experts (SMEs). By identifying potential scheduling conflicts and planned PTO well in advance, we lined up backup SMEs and adjusted timelines to prevent delays.

The Takeaway: Rigorous risk management isn’t about bureaucracy; it’s about foresight. By identifying and solving tomorrow’s problems today, you can transform potential crises into manageable tasks.

Lesson 3: Clear Scope Boundaries Are Your Best Defense

During a deep-dive session, a critical question emerged: Who was responsible for the long-term governance of the data entities being created? The project was building the house, but who would maintain it? This ambiguity around “Entity Governance” was a significant risk. If left unresolved, it could lead to data degradation and a lack of clear ownership post-launch.

It was tempting to absorb this into the project scope to “solve the problem,” but that would have led to scope creep and delays. Instead, we used the project’s governance structure to force a decision.

We formally escalated the issue to our Project Steering Committee (PAC), presenting it not as a project failure, but as a critical business decision needed for long-term success. The committee made a clear ruling: Entity Governance was officially out-of-scope for the project, and a specific business sponsor was named as the owner responsible for defining the long-term strategy.

The Takeaway: A project manager’s job is not just to deliver the project, but to ensure it can succeed in the long run. Sometimes, the most valuable thing you can do is to clearly define what you are not doing. Escalating scope ambiguities to leadership protects the project’s timeline and forces the organization to build the operational muscle needed for sustainable success.

Conclusion: Integration is a Team Sport

Ultimately, this project’s success came down to more than just technical solutions. It was a result of technical flexibility, rigorous risk management, and strong scope governance. By embracing adaptation, planning proactively, and seeking clarity, we delivered a solution that not only met the immediate business objectives but was also set up for long-term operational stability.
