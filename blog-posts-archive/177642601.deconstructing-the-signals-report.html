I was thrilled by the incredible response to my recent post on the “Project Signals Report.” The concept of a forward-looking, predictive intelligence briefing for projects clearly resonated.

The most common question I received was a simple but profound one: “This is brilliant, but how do you actually find these ‘leading risks’ before they hit your RAID log?”

The answer lies in learning to read the subtle, often-unspoken language of your project’s “digital exhaust”—the massive, unstructured stream of data generated in meeting transcripts, team chats, and emails. For years, only the most intuitive leaders could sense the risks hidden in this noise. Now, with a disciplined approach and modern analytical tools, we can all learn to systematically detect these patterns.

Here are three of the most powerful signals I look for to turn a project’s digital exhaust into a predictive intelligence stream.

1. Linguistic Decay: The Shift from “Will” to “Hope”

This is the most potent and reliable leading indicator of a future schedule slip. Pay close attention to the verbs your team members and vendors use.

  • A Confident Team Says: “We will deliver by the 15th.” “We can resolve that blocker.” “The fix is scheduled for Tuesday.” Their language is one of certainty and commitment.

  • A Wavering Team Says: “We are hoping to hit the 15th.” “We should be able to resolve that.” “We’re trying to get the fix scheduled.”

That subtle shift from “will” to “hope,” from “is” to “trying,” is a seismic event. It is a direct signal that their internal confidence has dropped, even if they haven’t declared a formal risk. They are verbally hedging their bets. A Signals Report quantifies this decay, alerting you to a drop in commitment weeks before the official “at risk” email is sent.

2. Keyword Frequency Spikes: The Rising Murmur

Every project has its own lexicon of problems. On a recent project, it was “database,” “latency,” and “performance.” At the start, these words were mentioned occasionally. But over a two-week period, the frequency of these keywords in meeting transcripts and technical chats began to spike.

This is the “rising murmur.” It’s the digital equivalent of hearing whispers about a problem in the hallway. Individually, each mention seems minor. But when analyzed in aggregate, a sharp increase in the frequency of problem-related keywords is a clear, data-driven signal that a minor issue is escalating into a potential show-stopper.

The Signals Report tracks these frequencies, quantifying the murmur before it becomes a roar. It allows you to ask the sharp question: “I’ve seen the term ‘database performance’ come up 25 times this week, up from 3 last week. What’s the real story here?”

3. The “Silence” Signal: What’s NOT Being Said

This is a more advanced but equally critical signal. Sometimes, the most dangerous risk is the one that is met with complete silence.

  • Topic Avoidance: You’re in a steering committee meeting, and you notice that no one is bringing up the difficult topic of data migration, even though it’s the next major phase.

  • Stakeholder Ghosting: A key business stakeholder who was once highly engaged suddenly stops responding to emails or attending meetings.

This is not a sign that the problem has gone away. It is a powerful negative signal of avoidance. It indicates a hidden blocker, a political disagreement, or a problem so complex that no one is willing to touch it. A good Signals Report can even track this, noting a “significant decline in communication” from a key individual or on a critical topic. It makes the silence visible.

The Lesson: Become a Data-Driven Intuitionist

These signals are ephemeral. They live in the subtext of conversations and the patterns of communication. In the past, we relied on our “gut feeling” to sense them.

Now, we can be more disciplined. By systematically capturing and analyzing our project’s digital exhaust, we can augment our intuition with data. We can learn to spot the linguistic decay, quantify the rising murmur, and see the silence. This is the future of program governance: moving from reactive risk management to predictive risk intelligence.
