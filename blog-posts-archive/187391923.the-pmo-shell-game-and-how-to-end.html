I recently witnessed a major enterprise PMO execute a masterclass in value destruction.
It wasn’t just incompetence. It was a structural retreat from reality.

Here is the anatomy of the collapse:

1. The Tooling Regression (Database to Desktop)
They migrated from an enterprise-grade dependency engine (Primavera) to a desktop-centric tool (MS Project).
The Failure: They traded a relational database for a file-based system.
Visibility vanished. Integration broke. The “schedule” became a drawing on a screen rather than a logic network.
Now, that tool is being decommissioned. Millions burned to go backward.

2. The Artifact Maze (Governance by Scavenger Hunt)
Governance was defined by where files lived, not what the data said.
SharePoint sites pointing to dead links. Templates changing weekly without version control.
The Failure: The “Standard” was never a constraint in the system; it was a document hiding in a folder.
Finding the rule took longer than following it.

3. The “Lightness” Fallacy
When the friction became unbearable, they claimed to make the process “light.”
They didn’t streamline execution; they just stopped asking for context.
The Failure: They confused removing friction with removing control.
Simplicity without structure isn’t agility. It’s ambiguity.

This is not governance.
This is an obstacle course.

If your Project Managers spend 40% of their week looking for a template or fixing a broken link, you are not managing projects. You are funding a bureaucracy.

The Pivot: The 2026 Standard

In 2026, we don’t “fix the links.” We burn the library.
Here is what a serious architectural pivot looks like:

1. From Artifacts to Data Streams
Stop managing documents (Word/PPT). Start managing data.
The “status report” shouldn’t be a creative writing exercise; it should be a live view generated by the system.
If you are uploading a file to prove you did the work, your architecture is obsolete.

2. Ambient Governance (Constraints, Not Coaching)
Rules shouldn’t be buried in a PDF on a SharePoint site.
Rules should be logic gates inside the platform.

  • Don’t ask a PM to “remember the budget threshold.”

  • Configure the system so they cannot enter a budget violation.
    If the user has to search for the governance, the governance has failed.

3. The AI Admin Layer
We have the technology today to automate the “Admin Tax.”

  • AI checks the schedule logic.

  • AI flags the compliance gaps.

  • AI writes the variance summary.
    Humans exist to adjudicate the red flags, not to manually format them.

The Bottom Line:

Intentional negligence is maintaining a digital environment that fights your people.

You have two choices:

  1. Continue playing the shell game with broken links and “light” ambiguity.

  2. Build a constraint-based engine where the admin disappears and the truth remains.

Fix the platform. Or get out of the way.
