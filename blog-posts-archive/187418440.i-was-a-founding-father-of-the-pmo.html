In 2013, I sat with a team of experts at PMI to codify the five standard PMO frameworks. I was there because I spoke a language few others did: The Physics of Control.

My professional DNA was forged in complexity—first as a Master Scheduler for the Pentagon Renovation, managing 30,000+ task dependencies, and then taming the Y2K and Euro-conversion chaos at Morgan Stanley.

The PMO We Tried to Build

The framework I advocated for—the Project Support/Controls Office—was built on the discipline of the Civil Engineer. I insisted it be the most rigorous function in the enterprise:

  • The Artifact: We enshrined the WBS (Work Breakdown Structure) and the Project Notebook as non-negotiable standards. Why? Because in construction, every beam, every rebar, every dollar must be accounted for. I demanded the same data structure for code and compliance.

  • The Mindset: I saw the PMO not as a Support function, but as a Controls function—a temporary SWAT team that installs the rigor (the Controls), executes the plan, and closes the office.

We wanted to build the Sensor Layer that gave clear visibility into the schedule and the moving pieces.

The Great Corruption

The tragedy is that the industry took our rigorous blueprint and corrupted the intent.

They took the Project Controls Office and turned it into the Potemkin PMO.

  • They kept the Form (the Charter, the Status Report) but deleted the Function (the Controls).

  • They replaced the objective truth of the WBS with the subjective lie of the “Green” dashboard.

The result is the structural disaster I witnessed years later: The company pays a fixer (me) to manage a multi-million dollar portfolio, but blocks me from the work. They don’t want management; they want a Human Shield to absorb the political risk.

The internal PMO became the worst of both worlds: It had the cost of rigor but delivered none of the value of truth.
